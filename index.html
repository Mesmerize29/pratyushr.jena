The next frontier of computer science

We have powerful models and fast hardware. The real frontier is how computer science behaves when it leaves the clean environment of a lab and enters the world of people, noise, and constraints.

My work sits at the intersection of AI security, systems, and cyber-physical behavior. I care about what actually happens when an algorithm meets reality: adversarial input, weird data, flaky networks, or a sensor that lies.

Whether it is an LLM, a perception pipeline, or a small autonomous platform, the question is the same: does this system still make sense when the world stops cooperating?

Patterns I chase

  • How a simple design decision ripples through a complex system.
  • Where vulnerabilities hide in interactions, not just in code.
  • How to borrow stability tricks from nature for CS systems.

Featured work

A few computer science projects that taught me something real about algorithms, security, and systems that have to work.

LLM security · Taxonomy

Interaction centric LLM security

Interaction driven cybersecurity for LLM powered systems

An ongoing research effort framing LLM security as a chain of interactions: inputs, internal processing, and outputs – not just “prompt tricks”.

I am building a taxonomy of failure and attack patterns and a layered defense pipeline that can be embedded into real systems.

  • Input sanitization and retrieval-guarding strategies.
  • Backdoor and behavior checks across the model pipeline.
  • Output auditing and risk scoring for downstream components.
Computer vision · Robotics handoff

Vial cap vision system

Vision guided vial cap detection and mapping

A RealSense based system that detects vial cap colors, segments them into holders A–D, and produces a clean data structure for a robot to use later.

Black caps, missing caps, and empty slots are treated consistently so the control logic downstream stays simple and robust.

  • Color segmentation that handles imperfect lab lighting.
  • Stable indexing for four 3×3 vial holders A–D.
  • Clear separation between perception and control layers.
Autonomy · Search pattern

Crazyflie search and avoid

Spiral search and obstacle-aware navigation (Crazyflie)

A micro-UAV that performs a spiral search to find a blue tape trail, locks on, and follows it while avoiding obstacles using optical flow and multiranger decks.

This project connected theoretical control ideas with real-world constraints like sensor noise, weight distribution, and unstable dynamics.

  • Spiral search logic implemented over noisy optical flow.
  • Simple, interpretable states for search, follow, and avoid.
  • Debugging loops based on logs, not guesswork.
Cyber-physical · LiDAR

Hexacopter + UGV behavior

Hexacopter with LiDAR and hybrid cyber-physical behavior

A custom hexacopter using Navio2, Garmin LiDAR Lite, TF Mini sensors, and Mission Planner – blending aerial navigation with cautious, ground-like behavior.

My focus here was more “computer science of the system” than hardware: modes, safety states, and how telemetry and logs can keep it explainable.

  • LiDAR-based environment awareness and mapping.
  • Mode switching logic expressed as discrete system states.
  • Telemetry-driven debugging and incident reconstruction.
