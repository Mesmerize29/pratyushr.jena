From raw curiosity to engineered intelligence

I never waited for perfect timing or perfect resources. I built my lane by taking on systems that looked complex from the outside and making them predictable from the inside. Every constraint became fuel. Every failure became data. Every challenge turned into a sharper execution loop.

My path across autonomous robotics, AI security, adversarial machine learning, and systems engineering is driven by a simple rule: if a system exists, its risks can be mapped; if its risks can be mapped, it can be secured; and once it is secured, it can scale with confidence.

Today I focus on engineering trustworthy AI, strengthening dialogue-level defenses for LLMs, and designing semi-autonomous robotic platforms that stay stable, observable, and useful in the real world — not just in a lab demo.

Research direction

My research sits at the intersection of AI safety, cybersecurity, and human-machine interaction. I study how language models behave under adversarial influence and how trust, misuse, over-reliance, and hidden vulnerabilities emerge in natural conversation.

I design frameworks that predict, measure, and mitigate failures at the dialogue level — aligning technical safeguards with human behavior so AI systems stay reliable under pressure, ambiguity, and targeted manipulation.

Core areas

  • Adversarial prompting, jailbreaks, and conversation-level exploits
  • Safety-aligned LLM system and UX design
  • Risk, misuse, and abuse-case modeling
  • Trust calibration and reliability metrics for LLMs

Next steps

Building toward a PhD in Computer Science focused on unifying LLM security frameworks with real-world system behavior, and scaling research that connects robotics, AI, and cybersecurity into one coherent practice.
